##Yanis Varoufakis: This Will Trigger Wall Street's Biggest Bubble Burst | LATEST INTERVIEW Trump Iran


In this interview, economist Yanis Varoufakis provides a critical analysis of the current state of Wall Street and the US dollar system, focusing on the recent $20 billion swap line announced by US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent for the Gulf States.
The Swap Line Puzzle (3:09 - 5:29)

Varoufakis argues that the mainstream narrative of a "bailout" for the Gulf States is incorrect. Instead, he views Scott Bessent's move as a symbolic gesture of panic. Bessent, a former hedge fund manager, is reportedly terrified that the "pack of wolves" in the financial markets are preparing to attack American bonds and stocks. The swap line is a message to financiers: the Treasury is prepared to print whatever is necessary to prop up the market.
Why the Flow Dried Up (0:00 - 3:08, 12:59 - 16:21)

Varoufakis explains the structural crisis behind this panic:

    The Pledges: In 2025, Gulf States (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait) pledged nearly $3 trillion in investments to the US—specifically $1.8 trillion in AI/tech and $1 trillion in weaponry. This was a massive "flow" of capital expected to enter Wall Street.
    The Reality: That flow has effectively vanished because Gulf liquidity has declined due to reduced oil revenues and challenges in diversified sectors like tourism and gold trading.
    The Impact: Bessent knows that losing a $1.5 trillion injection over 18 months in a market already defined by a "huge bubble" could trigger a massive crash.

Historical Context: The Global Surplus Recycling Mechanism (5:30 - 13:00)

Varoufakis contextualizes these events within the history of the US dollar:

    Bretton Woods (1944–1971): The US acted as a surplus nation, recycling dollars into Europe and Japan to maintain global demand.
    Post-1971: After becoming a deficit country, the US inverted this mechanism. It began weaponizing the dollar to ensure that the rest of the world’s capitalists recycled their surpluses back into Wall Street and US Treasuries to fund American deficits.

Conclusion: A Vicious Class War (16:22 - 18:01)

Varoufakis posits that the US is currently experiencing a "vicious class war" within the MAGA movement and corporate capitalism. While AI and financial sectors are being artificially propped up by the Treasury, the broader economy—particularly companies employing blue-collar workers—is being "bled dry." Regarding the potential end of US hegemony, he notes that while its eventual collapse is inevitable, predicting the exact timing is as impossible as timing the burst of a market bubble.

Yanis Varoufakis, the prominent economist and former Greek finance minister, views Japan as a highly instructive case study in global capitalism. He analyzes Japan through the lens of its shifting role within the US-led economic order, pointing out how the 1985 Plaza Accord forced the appreciation of the yen and subsequently triggered the domestic bubbles and decades of stagnation that plague the nation.

The Plaza Accord and the Lost Decades
Varoufakis describes the 1985 Plaza Accord as an ultimatum from Washington to Tokyo to massively appreciate the yen. He characterizes Japan's post-war rise as a pillar of the "Global Plan," where Japan acted as a crucial regional surplus country. When the United States faced growing trade deficits in the 1980s, it pressured Japan into the Plaza Accord to correct imbalances. According to Varoufakis, the resulting severe appreciation of the yen choked Japan's export-driven model. The Bank of Japan's subsequent attempts to use artificially low interest rates to stimulate the economy fueled a massive asset bubble, whose eventual collapse plunged Japan into decades of economic stagnation

The "Global Minotaur" and Capital Recycling
In his book The Global Minotaur, Varoufakis uses Japan as a critical example of how global capitalism recycled economic surpluses. After the breakdown of the Bretton Woods system, the United States ran massive trade deficits but absorbed surplus capital from around the world. Varoufakis points out that Japan, as a leading surplus nation in Asia, utilized this period to export capital goods and expand its corporate influence (keiretsu) across neighboring markets in South East Asia, spreading the reach of global capitalism

Japan as an Economic Precursor
Varoufakis has long warned that Japan's long-term struggles with deflation, zero-interest rates, and an aging population serve as a "crystal ball" for the rest of the developed world.
