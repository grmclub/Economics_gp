
-------------------------------------------------------------
World-Systems Theory, developed by Immanuel Wallerstein, argues that the global economy should be understood as a single interconnected capitalist system rather than as separate national economies. Instead of asking why some countries are rich and others poor, it examines how global inequality is produced through the unequal relationships between core, semi-periphery, and periphery regions.

Core countries dominate high-value production and finance, while peripheral countries often supply raw materials and cheap labor. The semi-periphery occupies an intermediate position, exploiting some regions while being exploited by others. Wallerstein argued that development and underdevelopment are not separate processes but two sides of the same world capitalist system.

Drawing on Marxism, Dependency Theory, and Fernand Braudel's historical sociology, World-Systems Theory challenged modernization theory by claiming that poverty in the Global South is largely the result of structural inequalities within global capitalism rather than internal cultural or institutional deficiencies.

Major Concepts: Core–Periphery, Semi-Periphery, World-Economy, Hegemony, Longue Durée, Kondratiev Waves, Antisystemic Movements.
