Japan Is Building a ‘Network of Navies’ Against Shared China Threat | Taiwan Talks

Japan is actively building a decentralized "network of navies" across the Indo-Pacific to deter Chinese maritime expansionism. By utilizing shared hardware, intelligence agreements, and arms diplomacy, Tokyo is forging a coalition of like-minded partners to secure critical sea lanes without relying on formal military alliances. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
Core Strategy and Key Partnerships
Instead of forming a rigid "Asian NATO," Japan is choosing to act as the connective hub for regional maritime forces. This allows partner nations to operate in unison through shared training, equipment, and maintenance networks.

    • Indonesia: Tokyo and Jakarta have engaged in talks to transfer retired Japanese Asagiri-class destroyers—warships equipped with anti-submarine helicopters and torpedoes. This improves Indonesia's ability to monitor foreign submarines near the critical Malacca and Lombok straits. 
	
    • The Philippines: Japan has agreed to transfer multiple Abukuma-class destroyer escorts to the Philippine Navy. Furthermore, the two nations have launched negotiations for a General Security of Military Information Agreement (GSOMIA) to securely share classified defense intelligence. 
	
    • Australia: Japan and Australia are undertaking a joint frigate development program, with Australia procuring 11 Mogami-class stealth frigates. This is Japan's largest-ever arms deal and its first de facto export of a destroyer-class warship. 
	
Drivers and Future Outlook
Japan’s 2026 defense white paper continues to name China as the country's most serious security concern. The strategy is highly supported, with recent polls indicating that 93% of Japanese citizens view China as a direct threat to national security.

By providing Southeast Asian partners with defense platforms, Japan ensures that countries along the First Island Chain can adequately protect their exclusive economic zones against Chinese gray-zone activities. This network heavily complements broader regional structures like the Quad alliance, allowing for seamless collaborative operations and enhanced deterrence against shared threats in the Indo-Pacific

A blue-water navy is a maritime force capable of operating globally, performing independent military operations across the deep waters of the open ocean far from its home ports.  The term is a key concept in maritime strategy, used to distinguish a nation's ability to project power across the high seas from navies limited to coastal or regional defense.

Classification of Navies
In naval parlance, navies are often categorized by their range and capability: 

==================================================================================
Classification     | Operational Scope
==================================================================================
Blue-Water Navy    | Open ocean, global reach, sustained operations.
Green-Water Navy   | Regional waters (coastal and near-seas); limited endurance.
Brown-Water Navy   | Littoral zones, riverine, and immediate coastal defense.

Yes, the Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force (JMSDF) is widely considered a blue-water navy.
While its official mandate is tied to the defense of Japan and its surrounding waters, its technical capabilities, equipment, and operational reach firmly place it in the category of a blue-water force.

Why the JMSDF is Classified as a Blue-Water Navy

Operational Reach and Logistics: The JMSDF possesses a sophisticated fleet of replenishment ships that allow its surface combatants to conduct sustained operations far from home ports. This capability is a fundamental requirement for blue-water status.

Advanced Surface Fleet: Japan operates a highly modern fleet, including Aegis-equipped destroyers (such as the Kongō, Atago, and Maya classes) that provide advanced air defense, anti-surface, and ballistic missile defense capabilities.

Helicopter Carriers: The Izumo-class and Hyūga-class "helicopter destroyers" act as significant power-projection platforms. The Izumo-class, in particular, has been modified to support fixed-wing STOVL (Short Take-Off and Vertical Landing) aircraft, further enhancing its blue-water profile.

Submarine Capability: The JMSDF maintains one of the world's most advanced submarine fleets (such as the Sōryū and Taigei classes), which are designed for long-endurance operations in open-ocean environments.

Interoperability: The JMSDF regularly conducts complex, large-scale, and multi-domain exercises with the U.S. Navy and other international partners across the Indo-Pacific. This high level of interoperability and participation in multinational operations demonstrates a capability well beyond simple coastal defense.

GCAP's potential expansion is a story about who gets to build sixth-generation airpower, and Japan's seat at that table is the real signal. Adding Canada or Saudi Arabia would spread costs, but it also internationalizes Japan's defense-industrial base in ways that quietly entangle European and Gulf interests with Indo-Pacific deterrence. If GCAP succeeds where the Franco-German effort failed, it sets a template, defense cooperation increasingly organized around capability and trust rather than geographic blocs, with Japan emerging as a credible co-architect of next-generation Western airpower rather than just a customer.
