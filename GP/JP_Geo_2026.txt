A blue-water navy is a maritime force capable of operating globally, performing independent military operations across the deep waters of the open ocean far from its home ports.  The term is a key concept in maritime strategy, used to distinguish a nation's ability to project power across the high seas from navies limited to coastal or regional defense.

Classification of Navies
In naval parlance, navies are often categorized by their range and capability: 

==================================================================================
Classification     | Operational Scope
==================================================================================
Blue-Water Navy    | Open ocean, global reach, sustained operations.
Green-Water Navy   | Regional waters (coastal and near-seas); limited endurance.
Brown-Water Navy   | Littoral zones, riverine, and immediate coastal defense.

Yes, the Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force (JMSDF) is widely considered a blue-water navy.
While its official mandate is tied to the defense of Japan and its surrounding waters, its technical capabilities, equipment, and operational reach firmly place it in the category of a blue-water force.
Why the JMSDF is Classified as a Blue-Water Navy
Operational Reach and Logistics: The JMSDF possesses a sophisticated fleet of replenishment ships that allow its surface combatants to conduct sustained operations far from home ports. This capability is a fundamental requirement for blue-water status.
Advanced Surface Fleet: Japan operates a highly modern fleet, including Aegis-equipped destroyers (such as the Kongō, Atago, and Maya classes) that provide advanced air defense, anti-surface, and ballistic missile defense capabilities.
Helicopter Carriers: The Izumo-class and Hyūga-class "helicopter destroyers" act as significant power-projection platforms. The Izumo-class, in particular, has been modified to support fixed-wing STOVL (Short Take-Off and Vertical Landing) aircraft, further enhancing its blue-water profile.
Submarine Capability: The JMSDF maintains one of the world's most advanced submarine fleets (such as the Sōryū and Taigei classes), which are designed for long-endurance operations in open-ocean environments.
Interoperability: The JMSDF regularly conducts complex, large-scale, and multi-domain exercises with the U.S. Navy and other international partners across the Indo-Pacific. This high level of interoperability and participation in multinational operations demonstrates a capability well beyond simple coastal defense.

If GCAP succeeds where the Franco-German effort failed, it sets a template, defense cooperation increasingly organized around capability and trust rather than geographic blocs, with Japan emerging as a credible co-architect of next-generation Western airpower rather than just a customer.
